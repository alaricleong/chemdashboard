(must balance C and H atoms) - Why cracking is important: converts less useful long-chain fractions into more useful short-chain fuels and alkene monomers for plastics Combustion of hydrocarbons: - Complete: hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (excess oxygen) - Incomplete: hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO + H₂O (or C + H₂O) (limited oxygen) - CO is toxic; C (soot) causes respiratory problems and is a pollutant Isomerism: - Structural isomers: same molecular formula but different structural arrangements - Example: C₄H₁₀ → butane (straight chain) and methylpropane (branched) - Branched isomers have lower boiling points than straight-chain isomers (less surface area → weaker van der Waals forces) Functional group summary: - Alkane: C–C single bonds only; general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ - Alkene: C=C double bond; general formula CₙH₂ₙ - Alcohol: –OH group; general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH - Carboxylic acid: –COOH group; general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH - Ester: –COO– linkage; formed from alcohol + carboxylic acid - Amine: –NH₂ group - Amide: –CONH– linkage (found in nylon, proteins) Ethanol production comparison: - Fermentation: - Raw material: glucose (from sugar cane/crops) — renewable - Conditions: yeast, 25–37°C, anaerobic (no oxygen), pH ~4–5 - Rate: slow - Purity: low (requires distillation to purify) - Batch process - Sustainable/renewable feedstock - Hydration of ethene: - Raw material: ethene (from cracking crude oil) — non-renewable - Conditions: steam, H₃PO₄ catalyst, 300°C, 60–70 atm - Rate: fast, continuous process - Purity: high - Not sustainable (uses fossil fuel feedstock) === STOICHIOMETRY & EQUATIONS === Writing balanced equations — steps: 1. Write word equation 2. Write correct formulae for all reactants and products 3. Balance by adjusting coefficients only (never change subscripts) 4. Add state symbols: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous Ionic equations: - Only include species that actually change (cancel out spectator ions) - Example: HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) Full ionic: H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + H₂O(l) Net ionic: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l) Half equations (for electrolysis and redox): - Cathode (reduction): Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu - Anode (oxidation): 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ - Balance: atoms first, then charge, then electrons Mole ratio from equation: - Coefficients in balanced equation = mole ratio - Example: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ 1 mol N₂ reacts with 3 mol H₂ to produce 2 mol NH₃ === NITROGEN CYCLE & HABER PROCESS === Haber Process (synthesis of ammonia): - Equation: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92 kJ/mol (exothermic) - Conditions: 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst - Compromise conditions explained: - Temperature: lower temp favours forward reaction (exothermic) and gives higher yield, but rate is too slow; 450°C is a compromise between rate and yield - Pressure: higher pressure favours forward reaction (fewer moles of gas on product side: 4→2), gives higher yield and faster rate; 200 atm is a compromise (higher pressure = expensive/dangerous) - Catalyst: iron catalyst increases rate without affecting equilibrium position; allows lower temperature to be used economically - Unreacted N₂ and H₂ are recycled back into the reactor (no waste) - Ammonia is liquefied and removed continuously Uses of ammonia: - Manufacture of fertilisers (e.g. ammonium nitrate NH₄NO₃, ammonium sulfate (NH₄)₂SO₄) - Manufacture of nitric acid (Ostwald process) - Cleaning products Contact Process (manufacture of sulfuric acid): - Step 1: S + O₂ → SO₂ (burning sulfur) - Step 2: 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (V₂O₅ catalyst, 450°C, 1–2 atm) - Step 3: SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ (oleum) then H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄ (SO₃ is NOT dissolved directly in water as it produces corrosive acid mist) - Uses of H₂SO₄: fertilisers, detergents, paints, car batteries, manufacture of other chemicals === EQUILIBRIUM === Dynamic equilibrium: forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate; concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal). - Only reached in a closed system Le Chatelier's Principle: if a change is made to a system at equilibrium, the system will shift to oppose that change. Effect of changes: - Increase concentration of reactant → equilibrium shifts RIGHT (forward) to use up added reactant - Decrease concentration of product → equilibrium shifts RIGHT - Increase temperature → equilibrium shifts in ENDOTHERMIC direction - Decrease temperature → equilibrium shifts in EXOTHERMIC direction - Increase pressure → equilibrium shifts towards side with FEWER moles of gas - Decrease pressure → equilibrium shifts towards side with MORE moles of gas - Adding a catalyst → does NOT shift equilibrium position; only increases rate of reaching equilibrium - Adding an inert gas at constant volume → NO effect on equilibrium position Kc expression: - For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ - Pure solids and liquids are NOT included in Kc expression - Kc only changes with temperature (not concentration, pressure, or catalyst) === RADIOACTIVITY (if applicable to syllabus) === Types of radiation: - Alpha (α): helium nucleus (²⁴He); charge +2; stopped by paper or a few cm of air; high ionising power; low penetrating power - Beta (β): fast-moving electron (⁰₋₁e); charge −1; stopped by thin aluminium sheet; medium ionising and penetrating power - Gamma (γ): electromagnetic radiation; no charge, no mass; stopped by thick lead or concrete; low ionising power; high penetrating power Nuclear equations: - Alpha decay: mass number decreases by 4, atomic number decreases by 2 Example: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He - Beta decay: mass number unchanged, atomic number increases by 1 Example: ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e Half-life: - Time taken for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay - After n half-lives: amount remaining = initial amount × (½)ⁿ - Half-life is constant and unaffected by temperature, pressure, or chemical state Uses of radioactivity: - Medical: gamma rays to sterilise equipment; radioactive tracers (e.g. iodine-131 for thyroid); radiotherapy (gamma rays to kill cancer cells) - Industrial: thickness gauges (beta), detecting leaks in pipes (gamma tracers) - Dating: carbon-14 dating of organic materials; uranium-lead dating of rocks Dangers of radiation: - Ionising radiation can damage DNA → mutations, cancer - Safety precautions: lead shielding, tongs/remote handling, minimise exposure time, dosimeters to monitor exposure === FINAL EXAM TIPS === Command words: - "State": give a fact with no explanation needed - "Explain": give a reason using scientific knowledge - "Describe": give an account of what happens (observations, not reasons) - "Suggest": apply knowledge to an unfamiliar situation;
